01 — Foundations

The Twelve Hallmarks of Aging

The hallmarks framework, established by López-Otín et al. (2013, updated 2023), organizes aging research into twelve mechanistic categories. Each hallmark represents a distinct but interconnected pathway of age-related deterioration.

Genomic Instability

High Evidence

Accumulation of DNA damage from endogenous and exogenous sources. Double-strand breaks, chromosomal aberrations, and telomere attrition contribute to cellular senescence and dysfunction.

Key interventions: DNA repair enhancement, genoprotective compounds

Telomere Attrition

High Evidence

Progressive shortening of telomeres with each cell division. Critical shortening triggers replicative senescence. Telomerase activity varies across tissue types and correlates with regenerative capacity.

Key interventions: Telomerase activation (experimental), lifestyle factors

Epigenetic Alterations

High Evidence

Age-related changes in DNA methylation patterns, histone modifications, and chromatin remodeling. Epigenetic clocks quantify biological age with high accuracy across tissues.

Key interventions: Partial reprogramming, epigenetic modulation

Loss of Proteostasis

High Evidence

Declining efficiency of protein synthesis, folding, and degradation systems. Accumulation of misfolded proteins and reduced autophagy contribute to neurodegenerative diseases and functional decline.

Key interventions: Autophagy induction, proteasome enhancement

Disabled Macroautophagy

Moderate Evidence

Age-related reduction in autophagy flux impairs cellular waste clearance. Mitophagy decline leads to accumulation of dysfunctional mitochondria. Autophagy stimulation extends lifespan in model organisms.

Key interventions: mTOR inhibition, caloric restriction, spermidine

Deregulated Nutrient Sensing

High Evidence

Dysregulation of insulin/IGF-1, mTOR, AMPK, and sirtuin pathways. Reduced metabolic flexibility and impaired response to nutrient availability. Strong animal evidence for longevity benefits of pathway modulation.

Key interventions: Metformin, rapamycin, NAD+ precursors

Mitochondrial Dysfunction

High Evidence

Declining mitochondrial efficiency, increased ROS production, and accumulation of mtDNA mutations. Impaired mitochondrial biogenesis and reduced oxidative capacity with age.

Key interventions: Exercise, mitochondrial-targeted antioxidants, NAD+ boost

Cellular Senescence

High Evidence

Accumulation of senescent cells secreting pro-inflammatory factors (SASP). Senescent cells resist apoptosis and contribute to tissue dysfunction. Senolytic interventions show promise in preclinical and early clinical studies.

Key interventions: Senolytics (dasatinib+quercetin, fisetin)

Stem Cell Exhaustion

Moderate Evidence

Age-related decline in stem cell number and regenerative capacity. Reduced tissue repair and homeostasis. Niche alterations impair stem cell function independent of intrinsic aging.

Key interventions: Young blood factors, rejuvenation protocols (experimental)

Altered Intercellular Communication

Moderate Evidence

Chronic inflammation (inflammaging), impaired neuroendocrine signaling, and altered extracellular matrix. Systemic factors influence aging across tissues via paracrine and endocrine mechanisms.

Key interventions: Anti-inflammatory compounds, parabiosis (experimental)

Chronic Inflammation

High Evidence

Low-grade systemic inflammation driven by SASP, immunosenescence, and gut dysbiosis. Elevated IL-6, TNF-α, and CRP correlate with age-related disease and mortality.

Key interventions: Lifestyle modification, senolytic therapy, rapamycin

Dysbiosis

Moderate Evidence

Age-related changes in gut microbiome composition and diversity. Reduced beneficial bacteria, increased pathobionts, and compromised gut barrier integrity. Links to systemic inflammation unclear.

Key interventions: Probiotics, prebiotics, dietary fiber (evidence mixed)

Citation: López-Otín C, Blasco MA, Partridge L, Serrano M, Kroemer G. Hallmarks of aging: An expanding universe. Cell. 2023;186(2):243-278. doi:10.1016/j.cell.2022.11.001

Partial Cellular Reprogramming

Preclinical Human trials 2025+

Transient expression of Yamanaka factors (OSK, OSKM) resets epigenetic age without dedifferentiation. Proof-of-concept demonstrated in mice (Ocampo 2016, Lu 2020). Altos Labs, Turn Bio, and others developing clinical applications.

Mechanism: Epigenetic clock reversal, cellular rejuvenation
Risk: Teratoma formation if dedifferentiation occurs
Timeline: First human trials expected 2025-2026

Stem Cell Therapies

Limited Clinical Data

Mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) and induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) for tissue regeneration. Safety established for MSCs; efficacy remains unclear. iPSC-derived cells show promise but face regulatory hurdles.

Mechanism: Tissue regeneration, paracrine signaling
Evidence: Mixed results in clinical trials
Status: MSCs approved for specific indications; longevity claims unproven

Mitochondrial Transfer

Experimental

Replacement of dysfunctional mitochondria with healthy mitochondria from young donors or autologous sources. Proof-of-concept in cell culture; in vivo delivery remains challenging.

Mechanism: Restoration of mitochondrial function
Challenge: Delivery method, immune response
Status: Early research stage

Telomerase Gene Therapy

Experimental

AAV-mediated TERT expression to restore telomerase activity. Demonstrated safety in mice without increased cancer incidence (Bernardes de Jesus 2012). Human applications face cancer risk concerns.

Mechanism: Telomere extension, enhanced proliferative capacity
Risk: Theoretical cancer promotion
Status: Preclinical only

Exercise

Strongest Human Evidence

Regular physical activity reduces all-cause mortality by ~30% (150 min/week moderate intensity). VO2max is strongest single predictor of longevity. Both aerobic and resistance training provide independent benefits.

Effect size: 1 MET increase → 10-25% mortality reduction
Mechanism: Multi-system (cardiovascular, metabolic, neurological)
Dose-response: Benefits continue beyond current guidelines

Caloric Restriction

Strong Animal Data Limited Human Data

20-40% caloric restriction extends lifespan in most model organisms. Human data from CALERIE trial shows metabolic benefits but no longevity endpoint. Compliance extremely difficult; potential for malnutrition and bone loss.

Animal effect: +20-40% lifespan extension
Human benefit: Unclear; surrogate markers improve
Practical limitation: Adherence, quality of life trade-off

Sleep Optimization

Strong Epidemiological Evidence

7-8 hours sleep duration associated with lowest mortality (U-shaped curve). Sleep fragmentation independently predicts cognitive decline and cardiovascular events. Circadian alignment critical for metabolic and immune function.

Optimal duration: 7-8 hours (both short and long sleep harmful)
Quality markers: Sleep efficiency, deep sleep percentage
Implementation: Consistent schedule, environment optimization

Social Connection

Strong Evidence

Social isolation increases mortality risk by ~50%, equivalent to smoking 15 cigarettes daily. Quality of relationships more important than quantity. Mechanisms include stress reduction, immune function, and health behaviors.

Effect size: Comparable to major clinical risk factors
Mechanism: HPA axis regulation, inflammation reduction
Evidence quality: Multiple large cohort studies

Parabiosis / Young Plasma

Experimental Only

Heterochronic parabiosis (young-old blood sharing) reverses aging markers in old mice. Young plasma infusions show mixed results. GDF11, oxytocin, and other factors implicated. Clinical trials by Alkahest showed no cognitive benefit in Alzheimer's patients.

Mechanism: Circulating rejuvenation factors vs. dilution of pro-aging factors
Status: Human trials disappointing; mechanism unclear
Recommendation: Not supported by evidence

Thymus Regeneration

Early Clinical Data

TRIIM trial (Fahy 2019): growth hormone, DHEA, metformin cocktail regenerated thymic tissue and reversed epigenetic age. Small n=9 study requires replication. Thymus regeneration could restore immune function in aging.

Result: 2.5 year epigenetic age reversal over 1 year
Limitation: Tiny sample, no control group, surrogate endpoint
Status: Larger trials needed

Hyperbaric Oxygen

Weak Evidence

Claims of telomere lengthening and senescent cell clearance from small Israeli study (Hachmo 2020, n=35). No replication; surrogate markers only. Mechanism unclear. Expensive intervention with no mortality or hard clinical endpoint data.

Evidence quality: Single small study, biomarker endpoints
Cost: Prohibitive for most individuals
Recommendation: Insufficient evidence to recommend

Cryonics

No Revival Technology

Preservation of legally dead individuals at -196°C with intent of future revival. Vitrification prevents ice crystal formation but causes chemical damage. No demonstrated ability to reverse preservation damage or revive complex organisms.

Status: Speculative; no proof of concept for revival
Organizations: Alcor, Cryonics Institute
Assessment: Unknown feasibility; last-resort option

03 — History

Longevity Research Timeline

Major milestones in aging biology and translational research. Timeline reflects established discoveries, not speculative projections.

1935

Caloric Restriction Discovery

Clive McCay demonstrates that caloric restriction extends lifespan in rats by up to 50%. Establishes dietary intervention as fundamental aging research paradigm.

McCay CM et al. J Nutr. 1935;10(1):63-79
1993

First Genetic Lifespan Extension

Cynthia Kenyon's lab discovers age-1 mutation doubles C. elegans lifespan. Demonstrates aging is genetically malleable, not fixed developmental program.

Kenyon C et al. Nature. 1993;366(6454):461-464
2000

Sirtuins and Longevity

Leonard Guarente identifies Sir2 (sirtuin) as mediator of caloric restriction benefits in yeast. Launches NAD+ and resveratrol research programs (later findings more nuanced).

Lin SJ et al. Science. 2000;289(5487):2126-2128
2009

Rapamycin Extends Mouse Lifespan

NIH Interventions Testing Program demonstrates rapamycin extends median lifespan by 14% in mice, even when started late-life. First drug shown to extend mammalian lifespan.

Harrison DE et al. Nature. 2009;460(7253):392-395
2011

Senescent Cell Removal Extends Healthspan

Baker et al. show genetic elimination of senescent cells delays age-related pathology in progeroid mice. Establishes senolytic therapy rationale.

Baker DJ et al. Nature. 2011;479(7372):232-236
2013

Hallmarks of Aging Framework

López-Otín et al. publish comprehensive framework organizing aging research into nine mechanistic hallmarks (later expanded to twelve in 2023 update).

López-Otín C et al. Cell. 2013;153(6):1194-1217
2013

Epigenetic Clocks

Steve Horvath develops DNA methylation-based aging clock accurate across tissues. Enables quantification of biological age and intervention testing.

Horvath S. Genome Biol. 2013;14(10):R115
2015

First Senolytic Drugs

Dasatinib + quercetin combination identified as senolytic. Launches clinical trial programs for age-related disease intervention.

Zhu Y et al. Aging Cell. 2015;14(4):644-658
2016

Partial Reprogramming Reverses Aging

Juan Carlos Izpisua Belmonte demonstrates transient Yamanaka factor expression reverses aging markers in progeroid mice without teratoma formation.

Ocampo A et al. Cell. 2016;167(7):1719-1733
2019

Thymus Regeneration Trial

TRIIM trial reports thymic regeneration and epigenetic age reversal with growth hormone + DHEA + metformin. Small study (n=9) requires replication.

Fahy GM et al. Aging Cell. 2019;18(6):e13028
2020

In Vivo Reprogramming Restores Vision

Sinclair lab demonstrates OSK reprogramming reverses glaucoma-induced vision loss in mice, resetting epigenetic age without dedifferentiation.

Lu Y et al. Nature. 2020;588(7836):124-129
2021

Altos Labs Founded

$3B cellular rejuvenation company launched with Yamanaka, Izpisua Belmonte, and others. Largest private investment in aging biology to date.

Industry milestone
2023

Hallmarks of Aging 2.0

Updated framework expands to twelve hallmarks, adding disabled macroautophagy, chronic inflammation, and dysbiosis based on decade of research.

López-Otín C et al. Cell. 2023;186(2):243-278
2024-2025

Current State

Multiple Phase II senolytic trials ongoing. Partial reprogramming approaches expected to enter human trials 2025-2026. TAME metformin trial enrolling. Translation gap from mice to humans remains primary challenge.

ClinicalTrials.gov registry

05 — Project

About VECVO

VECVO is a research compilation project focused on the biology of aging and lifespan-extending interventions. The project synthesizes peer-reviewed literature, ongoing clinical trials, and mechanistic research into the twelve hallmarks of aging.

All content is referenced to primary literature. Evidence strength is explicitly indicated using standardized categories: high (multiple RCTs or large cohorts), moderate (observational studies or limited trials), low (animal models only), preliminary (early-stage research). Speculative content is labeled as such.

The project prioritizes honest assessment of translational barriers, uncertainty acknowledgment, and distinction between established findings and ongoing research. No commercial affiliations or conflicts of interest.

Methodology

Content selection follows evidence hierarchy:

  1. Human randomized controlled trials (RCTs)
  2. Large observational cohort studies
  3. Mammalian model organism studies
  4. Short-lived model organisms (C. elegans, Drosophila)
  5. In vitro studies
  6. Theoretical mechanisms

Interventions are assessed on: strength of evidence, effect size, safety profile, practical implementation feasibility, and cost-benefit ratio. Translation uncertainty from model organisms to humans is explicitly discussed.
